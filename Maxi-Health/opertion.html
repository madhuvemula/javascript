Pre operative planning

  • On Admission Routine Blood Tests – Hb% , CBC, Blood Sugar, Blood Group Type, KFT, LFT, Serology, Urine Routine will be done.
  • Physician will examine the patient.
  • ECG and ECHO (if required) will be done and Cardiologist will check the patient for anesthesia fitness.
  • Gynecologist opinion will be obtained as and when required.
  • Anesthetist will do a complete pre -anesthetic evaluation of the patient.
  • Dental Examination will be obtained as and when required.
  • Blood and Blood Components are arranged depending upon the patient’s requirement.
  • Once the patient is fit for anesthesia and surgery implant / prosthesis options are given to the patient.

Implant / Prosthesis options

  • There are various implants available in the market .
  • Depending upon the patient requirements the artificial knee Prosthesis is choosen.
  • There are uni condylar prosthesis which is used for younger group of patients who have only half of the knee (Medial Compartment ) Osteo Arthritis.
  • Fixed bearing prosthesis is used for older group of patients.
  • There are Rotating platform , Hi flexion rotating platform , Oxenium knees and uncemented total knee prosthesis available.
  • Once the appropriate implant is choosen and patient is fit for anesthesia, the different anesthesia options are given to the patient.

Anesthesia options

  • Usually patients are given a combination of Spinal and Epidural Anesthesia.
  • Patient is operated under spinal Anesthesia and Epidural line is connected to an epidural pump which helps to maintain the patient pain free in the post operative period.
  • Rarely General Anesthesia is used-on patient’s Request.

Surgical Procedure

  • Depending on the patient requirements either a Single Total Knee Replacement or Bilateral Total Knee Arthroplasty is done – as planned for that particular patient.

Post Operative Care

  • Patient is allowed to rest during the first post operative day.
  • Drains are removed on the second post operative day and patients made to walk with the help of support.
  • Knee Exercises are started on the first post operative day.
  • Stair claiming and using of western toilet is started from the third or fourth post operative day.
  • Patients are discharged usually on the sixth or seventh post operative day.
  • Patients are advised Physiotherapy and walker support for two to three weeks after surgery.
  • Post Operative Exercises

Post Operative Exercises

  • Rehabilitation After Total Knee Replacement.
  • Knee replacement surgery causes weakness of the knee muscles, pain, inflammation and joint swelling, all important problems which the physiotherapist needs to treat promptly.

    • Goals

  • • Pain management (epidural, medication, rest, icepacks, compression and elevation)
  • • Muscle activation (isometric exercises)
  • • Restoring muscle control of the knee (ambulation and knee exercises)
  • • Gaining joint ROM (via reciprocal inhibition)
  • • Muscle bulk and strength (isotonic exercises)
  • • Balance and functional activities (proprioception)
  • • Encourage normal gait patterns and correct abnormal patterns (gait training)
  • • Tissue massage on the scar (massage)

    • Agenda

    Day 1 – 3
  • • Deep breathing exercises
  • • Pain management
  • • Toes / ankle movements
  • • Static quadriceps exercise
  • • Assist to sitting position / standing on bedside
  • • Weight bearing status as suggested / tolerate
    • Day 4 – 7
  • • Ambulation: FWB walking with walker support and PT assistance
  • • CPM of operated knee
  • • Knee exercises
  • • Begin climbing stairs with hand rails/banister and PT assistance
  • • Begin ADL with assistance
  • • Assessment for safe discharge
    • Physiotherapy treatment as an outpatient or house visit
  • • Work on the range of movement, Muscle bulk and strength
  • • Resistance is provided by manual technique and by using resistance bands.
  • • Concentrating on functional activities such as moving from sitting to standing and step ups and strengthening work with Theraband and the gymnastic ball.
  • • Work on range of motion will continue using resisted exercises and static bicycling and patients can usefully work on proprioception using the wobble board and other balance related activities
  • • Encourage normal gait patterns and correct abnormal patterns.
  • • The scar mobilisation by tissue massage daily.
    • Knee exercises
  • • Ankle pumps and circles
  • • Passive knee stretches for straight knee
  • • Static quadriceps exercises
  • • Sitting heel slides for knee bending
  • • Inner range quadriceps / short-arc-quads to strengthen knee extensors
  • • Knee hang to regain lost extension
  • • Resisted knee flexion exercise to increase ROM
  • • Straight leg raise
    • Do’s and Dont’s
  • • No pillow support under the operated knee
  • • Do not spend all your time exercising or your knee become inflamed, swollen and painful
  • • It needs a mixture of rest Regular exercises for instance 10 min exercise 6 to 8 times a day
  • • Monitor incision site and swelling
  • • Do not drive car for at least six weeks
  • • It is important that you continue your exercises for at least 3 months.
  • • You should continue to use your crutches/ walking stick until you can walk without a limp.

Trauma surgery is a surgical specialty involved in the invasive treatment of physical injuries, typically in an emergency setting.
Trauma surgeons like Dr Arumugam is responsible for the initial resuscitation and stabilization of the patient, as well as ongoing evaluation and management. The attending trauma surgeon also leads the trauma team, which typically includes nurses and support staff as well as resident physicians in teaching hospitals.
Most patients presenting to trauma centers have multiple injuries involving different organ systems, and so the care of such patients often requires a significant number of diagnostic studies and operative procedures.
The trauma surgeon is responsible for prioritizing such procedures and for designing the overall treatment plan. This process starts as soon as the patient arrives in the emergency department and continues to the operating room, intensive care unit, and hospital floor.
In most settings, patients are evaluated according to a set of predetermined protocols (triage) designed to detect and treat life-threatening conditions as soon as possible. After such conditions have been addressed (or ruled out), non-life-threatening injuries are addressed.
